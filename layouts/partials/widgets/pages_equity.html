In 2019, my disability self-advocacy and professional worlds collided after a dangerous run-in with autonomous delivery robots. This experience spurred my interest in exploring the how academic inaccessibly and quickly evolving, inaccessible tech feed into one another, and how best to remedy them without using disabled people as collateral.

Around the same time, I was heavily involved in the graduate student unionization campaign, and joined the Future of Research board of directors to lead the Labor Task Force, both of which drove my commitment toward addressing the long standing absence of fair and equitable labor practices in our science institutions, particularly for early career researchers.

Since, my commitment to equitable science has continued to grow. I have served as a member of two NASEM projects aiming to address disability issues and as a member of the NIH working group on the status of postdoctoral training in the US, and currently serve as the disability advisor for the systems biology department at Harvard. I have published writings and spoken publicly on my identity and experiences, and my vision for a more accessible scientific enterprise.
